## Current frontier

The remaining content of #6639 is the convergence proof: from
`x_0 = n` with fuel `2 * n.log2 + 1`, show the iteration reaches
the stop condition before fuel exhaustion. This requires either
(a) a two-phase invariant (geometric then quadratic descent) or
(b) an amortized "log2 decreases by ≥ 1 every 2 steps OR stop
triggers" claim specialized to iterations starting at `x_0 = n`.

The naive 2-step `Nat.log2` descent claim is provably false for
arbitrary starting `x`: a concrete counterexample is `n = 899,
x = 31 → x' = 30 → x'' = 29` with `Nat.log2` constant at 4
throughout. However, the actual iteration from `x_0 = 899` does
not pass through `x = 31` (it goes `899 → 450 → 225 → 114 → 60 →
37 → 30 → 29 → STOP`), suggesting that iterations rooted at
`x_0 = n` avoid the bad triples. Formalizing that "well-conditioned"
property is the open work tracked in #6649.

The parent issue's hint `x_k ≤ n/2^k + 2*k` also fails empirically
(n=1000, k=6: x_6=32, n/2^6=15, 2*6=12, sum=27 < 32), so the
two-phase approach with a tighter invariant is the more promising
direction.
