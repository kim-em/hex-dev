Two independent obstructions, the first decisive:

1. **Valuation conservation.** An exact identity `R_N = ±p^E·R_a` gives
   `v_p(R_N) = E + v_p(R_a)`. With the settled premise `v_p(R_a)=0`, this
   forces `E = v_p(R_N) ≥ k·d`, so the residual `p^{k·d−E} ∣ R_a` has
   exponent `≤ 0` — no positive p-power on the short object. The
   contradiction would then need `l2norm-product < p^0 = 1`, impossible
   for nonzero integer polys. Over-shortening raises `E`, strictly
   worsening it. The single cut diagonal `diag(p^{ℓ_j})` removes p-adic
   valuation and archimedean size with the *same* exponents, conserving
   the trade to exactly zero net.
2. **No scalar identity exists anyway.** Via `Res(f,a)=lc^{·}∏_i a(α_i)`,
   diagonal coefficient scaling gives a scalar multiple of the resultant
   for all `a` only if `p^{ℓ_j}` is geometric in `j`, i.e. `ℓ_j` affine.
   Binomial Mignotte `ℓ_j=⌈log_p(2B_j+1)⌉` is concave/middle-peaked, not
   affine. Plus the low residue `N_j = r_j + p^{ℓ_j}a_j` means
   `N ≠ D⊙aux` even at coefficient level.
